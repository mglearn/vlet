Jigsaw Method

A cooperative learning structure where every student becomes both a learner and a teacher, building foundational knowledge through structured peer expertise.
0.92
Effect Size
Surface Learning Deep Learning SOLO: Multistructural → Relational
The only strategy shown to be effective across both Surface and Deep learning phases — and the only one with the potential to double student learning in a single academic year when used consistently and correctly.

⚙️ How It Works — 5 Steps

  1. 1
    Form Home Groups (3–9 students). Assign each member a number corresponding to a unique content chunk.
  2. 2
    Expert Groups form — all students with the same number meet to deeply study their assigned content together.
  3. 3
    Experts master their content using readings, videos, or resources. They complete a Jigsaw Notes Organizer (key ideas, examples, quotes, applications).
  4. 4
    Return to Home Groups. Each expert teaches their section. Everyone learns the full picture through peer instruction.
  5. 5
    Repeat with refinement. Send students back to expert groups with clarification questions before a final debrief.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherDivide your next complex unit into 3–5 distinct content chunks. Create a Jigsaw Notes Organizer. Start with a paper version before moving to digital. Plan for at least 3 implementations — it takes practice to become routine.
🧑‍💼 CoachCo-plan the first Jigsaw lesson. Facilitate a SOLO calibration session where teachers analyze student expert-group notes. Model the strategy with staff during PD to build buy-in and reduce anxiety.
🏫 LeaderHighlight successful implementations in staff meetings. Build a resource bank of Jigsaw-ready content chunks across subject areas. Provide dedicated cross-curricular planning time.

Reciprocal Teaching

A structured dialogue strategy where students learn four comprehension strategies and gradually take ownership of the learning process — the "Fab Four."
0.74
Effect Size
Deep Learning SOLO: Relational
15–20 days of implementation can move reading comprehension from 30% to 70–80%, with gains maintained a full year later — across all ages, grade levels, and content areas.

🧩 The Fab Four

P
Predicting
Anticipate what comes next, activating prior knowledge and creating purpose for engagement.
Q
Questioning
Generate questions about content — moving beyond surface recall toward deeper analysis.
C
Clarifying
Identify confusing elements and use strategies to resolve misunderstandings.
S
Summarizing
Synthesize key information, distinguishing essential from non-essential details.

⚙️ Implementation Steps

  1. 1
    Teacher models each of the four strategies explicitly before students attempt them.
  2. 2
    Form groups of 4–5. Each student assumes one role: Predictor, Questioner, Clarifier, or Summarizer.
  3. 3
    Practice 15–30 minutes daily for 15–20 consecutive days to build the full effect.
  4. 4
    Rotate roles so students develop all four comprehension strategies over time.
  5. 5
    Extend beyond text — the Fab Four works equally well with videos, podcasts, and other media.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherCreate digital graphic organizers for each of the four roles. Use think-alouds to model each strategy first. Start with a short, accessible text before applying to complex content.
🧑‍💼 CoachCo-plan initial lessons. Help teachers develop differentiated sentence stems for each role. Observe and give specific feedback on gradual-release pacing.
🏫 LeaderProvide PD on all four strategies. Create a cross-curricular resource bank. Highlight implementations across subject areas in staff communications.

Concept Mapping

A visual learning strategy that helps learners organize, connect, and represent knowledge through structured diagrams — making thinking visible.
0.66
Effect Size
Deep Learning SOLO: Multistructural → Relational

💡 The BOOM Model

B
Brainstorm
Write down all key ideas related to the topic — no filtering yet.
O
Organize
Place the main idea at the center. Arrange supporting ideas around it in a hierarchy.
O
Open Connections
Draw lines between related ideas to show how they connect.
M
Make It Meaningful
Add linking words on connecting lines to explain each relationship explicitly.

📊 Map Types by SOLO Level

SOLO LevelBest Map Type
Pre-structural / UnistructuralBrainstorming maps, simple webs
MultistructuralHierarchical maps, flowcharts, concept sorts
RelationalNetwork maps showing cause-effect and dependencies
Extended AbstractComparative maps, Venn diagrams, cross-domain maps

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherModel map creation with a think-aloud. Provide partially completed maps for students to finish. Use concept maps as pre/post assessments to show knowledge growth across a unit.
🧑‍💼 CoachCo-plan a lesson with concept mapping as the central strategy. Help teachers connect different map types to SOLO levels. Share research on the impact of concept mapping on comprehension.
🏫 LeaderFacilitate PD on concept mapping across content areas. Encourage vertical alignment of mapping techniques K–12. Create a library of exemplar maps for teachers to reference.

Problem-Solving Teaching

A structured approach to problem-based learning, implemented during the Transfer phase after students have built foundational and conceptual knowledge.
0.61
Effect Size
Transfer Learning SOLO: Extended Abstract
Timing is everything. Problem-solving teaching only reaches its potential after students have built strong surface and deep knowledge. Deployed too early, it falls flat.

🌈 Design Problems Using PRISM

P
Patterns
What patterns do you notice? What do they suggest about underlying causes?
R
Reasoning
How do pieces of information connect logically? What cause-and-effect relationships exist?
I
Ideas
What multiple approaches or solutions are possible? Generate options before evaluating.
S
Situation
What is the broader context? Who is affected? What real-world constraints apply?
M
Methods
How will you evaluate the solution? What criteria matter? How could you test it?

⚙️ Designing Effective Problems

  1. 1
    Confirm students have mastered surface and deep learning first — use SOLO to verify.
  2. 2
    Make problems authentic and relevant — connected to real situations students can recognize.
  3. 3
    Ensure multiple solution paths exist — no single right answer.
  4. 4
    Use PRISM to structure student thinking throughout the challenge.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherDesign one rich problem per unit using PRISM. Pair with Think-Pair-Share + Peer Mentoring (ES: 0.74). Require students to show reasoning, not just their final answer.
🧑‍💼 CoachHelp teachers distinguish simple application tasks from true transfer. Model PRISM in planning sessions. Use SOLO to evaluate whether a problem genuinely reaches Extended Abstract.
🏫 LeaderAllocate time for extended problem-solving. Showcase transfer learning examples. Encourage cross-curricular problems that require knowledge from multiple disciplines.

Critical Thinking

The process of analyzing, synthesizing, and evaluating information to guide beliefs and actions — essential for both deep and transfer learning.
0.79
Effect Size
Deep Learning Transfer Learning
"The brain works by taking shortcuts, deceiving us into seeing things that aren't there and believing things that aren't true. Critical thinking is the antidote."

🔍 Five Core Components

  1. A
    Analysis — Break down complex information into components. Examine relationships between parts.
  2. E
    Evaluation — Assess the credibility, relevance, and quality of evidence and arguments.
  3. S
    Synthesis — Combine ideas from multiple sources to create new understanding.
  4. I
    Inference — Draw logical conclusions from available evidence.
  5. E
    Explanation — Clearly articulate reasoning and justify conclusions to others.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherExplicitly teach FLOATER and SIFT (see sidebar). Apply them to evaluate claims, ads, AI content, and news. Create a classroom culture where "What's your evidence?" is a normal question.
🧑‍💼 CoachHelp teachers build questioning sequences that push from recall to analysis. Co-design tasks requiring students to justify their thinking in writing or discussion.
🏫 LeaderMake FLOATER and SIFT part of campus vocabulary. Integrate media literacy into PD. Highlight student critical thinking examples in walkthrough debrief conversations.

See FLOATER and SIFT in the sidebar for step-by-step guides you can use directly with students.

Teacher Clarity

When teachers communicate learning intentions, success criteria, and lesson purpose with precision, students learn significantly faster across all phases.
0.85
Effect Size
All Learning Phases

⚙️ Three Pillars

  1. 1
    Learning Intentions — "What will I learn today, and why does it matter?" State these clearly in student-friendly language before instruction begins. Frame them using SOLO levels.
  2. 2
    Success Criteria (ES: 0.64) — "How will I know when I've learned it?" Provide specific, observable criteria students can self-assess against.
  3. 3
    Relevance — Connect the learning to students' lives, prior knowledge, or future goals to activate motivation and meaning.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherPost learning intentions and success criteria every day in student-friendly language. Revisit them mid-lesson and at the close. Ask students: "Where are you in relation to today's success criteria?"
🧑‍💼 CoachDuring walkthroughs, look for evidence that students can articulate the learning intention in their own words. Co-create success criteria templates aligned to SOLO levels.
🏫 LeaderInclude learning intentions and success criteria in walkthrough protocols. Use Teacher Clarity principles to communicate school-wide instructional expectations. Give PD goals clear success criteria — model what you expect.

Argumentation

Students construct and defend evidence-based claims, directly engaging with conflicting perspectives to deepen conceptual understanding.
0.86
Effect Size
Deep Learning SOLO: Relational → Extended Abstract

⚙️ Claim-Evidence-Reasoning (CER)

  1. C
    Claim — A clear, testable statement that directly answers the question or addresses the problem.
  2. E
    Evidence — Specific data, text evidence, or observations that support the claim.
  3. R
    Reasoning — An explanation of why the evidence supports the claim, connecting to underlying principles.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherTeach CER structure explicitly with graphic organizers before students use it independently. Use structured debate protocols. Require counter-arguments to push toward Extended Abstract thinking.
🧑‍💼 CoachHelp teachers build questioning sequences ("What's your evidence? How does that support your claim?"). Co-build a cross-curricular bank of argumentation prompts.
🏫 LeaderCreate a campus-wide focus on argumentation across all disciplines — not just ELA. Develop cross-curricular resources. Provide PD on designing tasks that require evidence-based reasoning.

Self-Judgment and Reflection

When students evaluate their own learning against clear criteria, metacognitive awareness grows and learning accelerates across all phases.
0.81
Effect Size
All Learning Phases
"Reflection bridges theory and practice. It creates ownership over learning and transforms information into implementation."

⚙️ Practical Approaches

  1. 1
    Self-assess against success criteria — Students rate themselves on SOLO-anchored criteria. Where am I? Where do I need to go?
  2. 2
    Exit tickets — One thing learned, one question remaining, one thing to apply. Quick and high-value.
  3. 3
    Learning journals — Regular written reflection on strategies used, what worked, and next steps.
  4. 4
    Peer feedback first — Structured peer review using agreed-upon criteria before individual self-assessment.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherBuild daily reflection routines using SOLO language. Give students time to revise work after self-assessment. Don't just collect reflections — respond to them.
🧑‍💼 CoachModel reflective practice with teachers in EIIR Reflect & Realize conversations. Help teachers design prompts tied to specific learning intentions.
🏫 LeaderBuild structured reflection into staff meetings and PLC agendas. Treat reflection as a measurable part of professional learning — not just a closing activity.

Collective Teacher Efficacy

The shared belief among a faculty that their combined efforts can produce positive outcomes for every student — the single highest-impact factor in Visible Learning research.
1.01
Effect Size
Campus-Wide All Phases
"When teachers collectively believe they can impact student learning, students perform dramatically better. This is the #1 factor in the Visible Learning research."

💡 How to Build It — By Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherParticipate actively in PLCs. Share what worked — and what didn't. Celebrate student growth publicly. Use common instructional language (SOLO, ALDO, PRISM) to collaborate across classrooms.
🧑‍💼 CoachFacilitate teacher-to-teacher sharing of high-impact strategy implementations. Create structures for peer observation and debrief. Use Video Microteaching (ES: 0.99) to create collaborative reflection cycles.
🏫 LeaderImplement monthly data meetings with structured protocols focused on student growth — not just scores. Share success stories school-wide. Make collective commitments visible through RISE goals. Remove barriers to collaborative planning.

Video Microteaching

Recording, reviewing, and reflecting on short teaching segments — one of the most powerful professional learning approaches available.
0.99
Effect Size
Professional Learning

⚙️ The Record–Reflect–Improve Cycle

  1. 1
    Plan — Identify one specific strategy to focus on (e.g., delivering learning intentions). Set clear success criteria for the observation.
  2. 2
    Record — Capture a 5–15 minute teaching segment. Focus matters more than length.
  3. 3
    Self-review — The teacher watches privately first, using the success criteria as a lens.
  4. 4
    Collaborative debrief — Share with a coach or PLC partner using structured protocols. Focus on student behavior, not teacher performance.
  5. 5
    Improve and repeat — Implement one specific change. Record again within 1–2 weeks.

💡 Classroom Implementation by Role

RoleWhat to Do
👩‍🏫 TeacherCommit to one recording cycle per month. Review privately before sharing. Key questions: "When were students most engaged? When did learning appear to stall?"
🧑‍💼 CoachGuide 5 teachers through a full record/reflect/improve cycle. Build psychological safety before introducing video. Keep debrief focus on evidence of student thinking — not teacher evaluation.
🏫 LeaderCreate a culture where video reflection is normal and non-evaluative. Provide the time and technology. Build credibility by recording your own practice first.

SOLO Taxonomy

Structure of the Observed Learning Outcome — a research-based framework for understanding how learning develops in complexity from surface to transfer.
Assessment Framework Learning Progression
"The greatest value of SOLO is that it shows how our learning develops — it gives teachers a shared language for talking about student thinking."

📊 The Five Levels

Pre-StructuralBefore formal instruction. Prior knowledge may be inaccurate or irrelevant. The student response misses the point entirely.
Example: Asked about deforestation, student says "Trees are green."
Unistructural — Surface LearningStudent grasps one relevant idea but cannot connect it to anything else or apply it further.
Example: "Cutting trees causes deforestation." — one cause, no connections.
Multistructural — Surface LearningStudent knows several relevant facts but sees them as separate and unconnected.
Example: Lists "cutting trees, agriculture, and fires" as causes — but can't explain how they interrelate.
Relational — Deep LearningStudent connects ideas, explains relationships, and can reconcile conflicting information into a coherent whole.
Example: Explains how human activity, climate, and economics interact to drive deforestation.
Extended Abstract — Transfer LearningStudent generalizes to new contexts, creates hypotheses, and applies principles to novel problems they have not seen before.
Example: Designs a multi-factor policy solution drawing on ecology, economics, and social science.
Approximately 82% of classroom teaching remains at surface learning level. SOLO helps teachers intentionally design for deeper progression.

⚠️ Important Distinction

A student's verbal understanding may be at a higher SOLO level than their written work. This gap may reflect a writing skill barrier, vocabulary challenge, or language issue — not a lack of understanding. Always investigate before drawing conclusions.

ALDO — Amazing Lesson Design Outline

A comprehensive lesson design framework integrating brain-based learning, SEL, high-effect size strategies, and educational technology.
Lesson Design Technology Integration

⚙️ Five Components

  1. 1
    Build Relationships — Establish trust and safety through SEL and brain-based learning. Students learn best when they feel safe and valued. This step is non-negotiable.
  2. 2
    Pre-Assess Students — Use low-stakes assessments to determine current SOLO level. This guides strategy selection, grouping, and pacing decisions.
  3. 3
    Select Strategies — Choose high-effect size strategies matched to the appropriate learning phase (Surface, Deep, or Transfer) and your learning intention.
  4. 4
    Post-Assess — Gauge effectiveness and provide targeted feedback. Compare to pre-assessment to measure growth and inform next steps.
  5. 5
    Reflect and Share — Encourage metacognition for both teacher and learner. What worked? What would you change? What did students learn about their own learning?
Core principle: Strategy first, then technology. Always identify the evidence-based strategy and its learning phase before selecting a digital tool. Ask: "Does this tool amplify the strategy — or just add complexity?"
